Financial Pressures

Financial insecurity & housing expenses

Financial insecurity, driven largely by taxes, is among the top concerns for older residents.

Renters age 65+ spending 30%+ of income on housing (highest of any age group)50%
Need home repairs to safely remain over the next 5 years45%
Wrote in about cost-of-living concerns (maintenance, taxes, utilities)37%
Lacked finances for at least one basic necessity in the past 12 months20%
Of those needing repairs, could not afford them14%

Each bar reflects its own group as reported in the survey; denominators differ (e.g., renters 65+, all respondents, those needing repairs).

  • Women living alone have a median income of just $57,500 — older adults living alone have substantially lower incomes.
  • Stakeholders described many residents as "house rich but tax poor," warning that longtime residents may be priced out.
"Taxes are crushing us; cannot keep up with increases; falling behind on paying for services (water, trash, electric, heat expenses)" — Brookline survey respondent

Getting Around

Transportation & accessibility

Focus group participants repeatedly identified medical transportation as a core service that supports independence and aging in place — and voiced concern about reductions in it.

16%
of non-driving respondents missed, cancelled, or rescheduled a medical appointment because of transportation barriers
Nearly ½
of respondents reported at least one transportation challenge
  • Reductions in medical transportation services
  • Not enough subsidized rideshare or medical-transport rides
  • Unsafe sidewalks and winter conditions
  • Homes & community spaces hard to navigate with walkers/wheelchairs

Social Connection

Social wellness & isolation

Older adults who live alone may especially benefit from programs and services that offer opportunities to connect in person.

42%
of respondents age 80+ live alone (vs. 25% of those in their 60s)
19%
reported some form of exclusion — 33% of them cited age as the reason
37.8%
depression rate among Brookline older adults, higher than the state (34.6%)
"Isolation. Since I live alone in my own house. Neighbors tend to be younger with busy lives. Older friends/neighbors are moving away, getting sick/dementia, etc. Can create 'loneliness'. Which makes having a great + accessible Senior Center so important." — Brookline survey respondent

Brookline Senior Center

An invaluable resource — at capacity

Residents want the Brookline Senior Center to keep expanding options for participation, but its capacity to serve the diverse needs of older residents is limited.

Have attended the Senior CenterNot yet reached
34% attended
66% not yet reached

34% of survey respondents have attended the Senior Center; participation is more common among older residents.

35% of monthly attendees say more parking would boost participation 32% of occasional attendees say the same 37% want more weekend programming
  • Opened in 2001, when about 9,028 residents were 60+ — compared with about 13,347 residents 60+ in 2024.
  • Strong interest in expanded educational, cultural, and wellness programming, pointing to demand for more space.
"Senior center is too small for the number of elderly who live in Brookline… Space is inadequate. A much larger building is needed with parking!" — Brookline survey respondent

Caregiving

The strain on family caregivers

Older residents experience changing physical and psychological conditions, and caregivers to older adults need help managing this care.

41%
of respondents provided care or assistance to a disabled or frail person in the past 5 years
50%
of caregivers are ages 60–69 — the largest share of any cohort, suggesting many are part of the "sandwich" generation
57%
of caregivers found it somewhat or very challenging to balance with other responsibilities

Approximately 1 in 5 caregivers reported declines in any of the following due to caregiving:

  • Physical health
  • Mental health
  • Social life

The Path Forward

7 priorities for action

Strategic recommendations for the Town of Brookline and the Council on Aging. Filter by level, and expand any card for the full recommendation and its supporting data.

Critical

Financial security & tax relief

Ease the property-tax burden on older homeowners.

Continue reviewing and expanding property tax relief programs to help older homeowners remain in their homes.

50% of renters age 65+ are housing cost-burdened

Critical

Outreach & in-home supports for 80+

Reach isolated adults 80 and older at home.

Prioritize outreach and in-home supports for adults 80 and older, especially those living alone or without nearby family.

42% of respondents age 80+ live alone

Critical

Restore & expand transportation

Protect rides to medical care.

Prioritize restoring and expanding transportation services that help older adults reach healthcare appointments.

16% of non-driving respondents missed or delayed medical care

High Priority

Expand caregiver support

Support family caregivers, including younger ones.

Expand support for family caregivers and identify pathways to reach "younger older adults" who would benefit from caregiving support.

41% are caregivers; 57% find it challenging

High Priority

Accessibility & Age-Friendly design

Make public spaces navigable for all.

Review municipal facilities, destinations, and public spaces, and continue to incorporate Age-Friendly principles across all departments and boards.

39% of residents age 75+ report a disability

High Priority

Long-term facilities strategy

Plan a Senior Center that fits demand.

Develop a long-term strategy — including renovation, expansion, or alternative and satellite service delivery models — so more residents can be served.

Only 34% of respondents have ever attended

Important

Coordinated communications hub

Make it easy to find programs and services.

Reposition the Senior Center as a central hub for information and engagement using multiple platforms — print, digital, and telephone.

55% of non-attendees want more program information

The Opportunity

If these recommendations are implemented

Longtime residents stay in their homes. Expanded property tax relief helps older homeowners remain, while easing pressure on the half of renters 65+ who are cost-burdened.

Isolated older adults get connected. Targeted outreach reaches the 42% of respondents age 80+ who live alone.

Fewer missed medical appointments. Restored transportation supports the 16% of non-driving respondents who had to skip or reschedule care.

Caregivers get relief. New supports reach the 41% of residents who provide care — and the 57% who find it challenging.

A Senior Center that fits the community. A facilities strategy addresses a Center built when 9,028 residents were 60+, now serving about 13,347.

More residents find their way in. Coordinated outreach engages the 55% of non-attendees who simply want to know what's offered.

In conclusion

Brookline's older population will keep expanding, and demand for supportive services will rise alongside it. Acting on these priorities lets the town lean into the strengths it already has while directing attention and resources to where older residents need them most.

"Brookline is great and could do so much more. The few times I have visited the Senior Center to see what is offered I have felt like I entered an assisted living facility." — Brookline survey respondent
